# Draft Abstract

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is becoming increasingly relevant for official statistics, particularly in tasks that require the transformation of unstructured textual information into structured statistical variables. In official statistics, structured classifications have always played a central role, since they provide a shared statistical language for ensuring consistency, comparability, and integration; in this context, text classification becomes a key operational task for linking unstructured textual information from administrative and non-traditional digital sources to official statistical categories. Compared with traditional rule-based approaches, recent advances in natural language processing, including sentence embeddings, semantic search, and large language models, offer greater flexibility in handling evolving classifications, heterogeneous textual descriptions, and new data sources.

This paper presents Istat's approach to AI-based text classification in official statistics through two current use cases: the automatic coding of economic activity descriptions into ATECO categories and the classification of products into PRODCOM codes using company websites as auxiliary information sources. These cases show how AI-based methods can improve adaptability, reduce reliance on manually maintained vocabularies, and support more scalable coding workflows. At the same time, they highlight important methodological and operational issues related to validation, statistical quality, transparency, traceability, confidentiality, and integration into existing production processes.

From this perspective, the paper argues that the adoption of AI for text classification in official statistics cannot be treated solely as a matter of model performance. It requires a governance framework capable of defining priorities, responsibilities, validation procedures, and operational safeguards for the design and deployment of AI applications. The contribution therefore discusses Istat's emerging governance approach as a way to ensure that methodological innovation remains aligned with the quality principles and institutional obligations of official statistics.
