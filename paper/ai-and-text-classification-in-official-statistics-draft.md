# AI and Text Classification in Official Statistics

In official statistics, text classification is not a marginal technical task, but a core operational function. Many statistical processes depend on the transformation of textual descriptions into structured variables that are consistent with official classifications such as ATECO, NACE, ISCO, or PRODCOM. This need arises in several contexts, including the coding of economic activities, the classification of products, and the integration of information extracted from administrative sources and non-traditional digital data. As statistical production increasingly interacts with heterogeneous and unstructured textual material, the ability to classify text accurately and consistently becomes essential for harmonization, comparability, and downstream statistical use.

Traditionally, these tasks have been addressed through deterministic systems based on dictionaries, lexical rules, normalization procedures, and manually curated vocabularies. Such systems can be effective in stable and well-controlled environments, but they are often difficult to maintain when classifications evolve, terminology changes, or new data sources introduce more heterogeneous language. This is one of the main reasons why recent advances in artificial intelligence, and particularly in natural language processing, have become relevant for official statistics. Embedding-based models, semantic search systems, and large language models make it possible to move beyond exact word matching and to classify texts on the basis of semantic similarity. This is especially important when textual descriptions are short, incomplete, noisy, or only partially aligned with the formal wording of statistical classifications. In these settings, AI-based methods can improve flexibility, scalability, and adaptability, while reducing dependence on manually maintained rule systems and static vocabularies.

At the same time, the relevance of AI for text classification in official statistics cannot be assessed in terms of technical performance alone. Classification outputs may influence core statistical processes and therefore must remain compatible with established requirements concerning quality, transparency, traceability, confidentiality, and human oversight. For national statistical institutes, this means that AI-based text classification should be understood not simply as an automation tool, but as a methodological and governance issue. What matters is not only whether AI improves coding accuracy or efficiency, but whether it can be integrated into statistical production in a way that remains explainable, controllable, auditable, and institutionally defensible. In line with the broader risk-based orientation emerging in current AI regulation, the adoption of AI for text classification requires robust validation procedures, clear accountability, and safeguards that preserve the fundamental principles of official statistics while enabling innovation in concrete operational contexts.

Against this background, the following use cases illustrate how Istat is approaching AI-based text classification in practice. They show that methodological innovation is being explored in areas where text classification is operationally relevant, while at the same time remaining embedded within requirements of validation, control, and institutional responsibility.

## 2.1 Use Case: ATECO Automatic Coding

One relevant use case concerns the automatic coding of economic activity descriptions into ATECO categories. This task is central in official statistics because textual descriptions collected from enterprises are often short, heterogeneous, and only partially aligned with the formal vocabulary of the classification. Traditional coding systems based on deterministic rules and lexical matching can provide useful support, but they require extensive manual maintenance and may struggle when terminology changes or when firms describe their activity in non-standard ways. For this reason, recent work has explored semantic-search-based approaches in which textual descriptions and official category definitions are represented in a shared semantic space through sentence embeddings. This makes it possible to retrieve candidate codes on the basis of meaning rather than exact keyword overlap, thereby improving flexibility and making the coding process more adaptable to evolving real-world descriptions.

In practice, the ATECO use case is especially relevant because it mirrors a recurrent difficulty in official statistics: the inferential target is defined by a stable formal classification, whereas the observed input consists of user-like descriptions that are noisy, incomplete, and linguistically variable. This asymmetry makes purely lexical matching fragile, particularly when the same activity can be described through different expressions or when subtle differences in wording correspond to distinct categories. Embedding-based retrieval is useful precisely because it helps bridge this gap between institutional taxonomies and real-world language. It can support candidate generation more effectively than manually maintained vocabularies alone, and it is better suited to contexts where classifications are periodically revised and where new descriptions emerge continuously from reporting units.

As illustrated in Figure 1, the difference between the lexical and semantic approaches is not merely technical, but methodological. In rule-based systems, classification depends heavily on explicit vocabularies and direct textual overlap, whereas semantic approaches operate on representations that capture similarity at the level of meaning. This makes the latter more robust when enterprise descriptions diverge from the canonical wording of the classification and helps explain why semantic retrieval is increasingly relevant for coding tasks in official statistics.

At the same time, the ATECO case highlights why AI-based classification in official statistics must be embedded in a broader methodological framework. The issue is not only whether the correct code appears among the top candidates, but also whether the resulting suggestion is sufficiently reliable to support official statistical operations. This requires validation against baseline systems, expert assessment, and, where possible, explicit uncertainty handling. More generally, the ATECO experience suggests that AI should be understood as decision support for coding activities rather than as an unconstrained automation device. Its practical value lies in improving candidate selection, reducing manual effort, and making coding systems more adaptable, while preserving the need for traceability and methodological control. In this sense, the ATECO use case is representative of a wider transition from deterministic coding systems toward semantic and AI-supported approaches, while also showing that statistical defensibility remains a necessary condition for operational adoption.

## 2.2 Use Case: From Company Websites to Official Product Codes

A second relevant use case concerns the classification of products into PRODCOM codes using company websites as auxiliary information sources. In business surveys, product coding is often burdensome because product descriptions are heterogeneous, incomplete, and highly context-dependent. In this setting, web data can support statistical production by providing additional evidence on the goods and services associated with a given enterprise. Recent work has proposed a modular framework in which company websites are first identified, then filtered to retain product-relevant pages, and finally used to extract product evidence that can be mapped to official PRODCOM codes. Within this architecture, different coding strategies can be adopted, including embedding-based retrieval, LLM-assisted classification, optional disambiguation procedures, and supervised models.

The PRODCOM case is methodologically important because it makes explicit that text classification in official statistics may depend on an upstream evidence-construction process. Before a code can be assigned, the system must identify the relevant website, detect the pages that actually contain product information, and extract textual fragments that are informative for classification. In other words, the coding task is part of a staged pipeline rather than a single isolated prediction problem. This is a useful reminder that, in operational settings, classification quality depends not only on the final model but also on the quality and relevance of the textual evidence that is collected and filtered along the way. For official statistics, this modular structure is also advantageous because it improves traceability: each stage can be inspected, evaluated, and refined separately.

Even in simplified form, it helps make visible a key point of the PRODCOM use case: the classification output is the result of several connected stages, from website identification to evidence extraction and final code assignment. This staged structure is one of the reasons why the approach is relevant for official statistics, since it supports modular evaluation and makes the workflow easier to inspect and govern.

The PRODCOM case is also important because it shows that AI-based text classification in official statistics is not limited to assigning labels to isolated text strings. Rather, it can be part of a broader data-driven pipeline that integrates web intelligence, evidence extraction, and coding support into survey operations. In this perspective, AI functions as a digital assistant for statistical offices, helping reduce response burden, support questionnaire pre-filling, and improve coding validation. The use case therefore illustrates a particularly concrete form of operational value: classification is linked to survey support, more efficient data collection, and better use of already available digital information. At the same time, the pipeline remains compatible with the broader institutional logic of official statistics, since its outputs can be reviewed, validated, and incorporated into controlled production processes. This makes the PRODCOM use case particularly relevant for discussing the practical value of AI in official statistics: it demonstrates how methodological innovation can be connected to concrete gains in efficiency and scalability, while still preserving the need for traceable, controllable, and institutionally governed workflows.

## 2.3 Risks, Constraints and Methodological Requirements

The two use cases discussed above show that the adoption of AI-based text classification in official statistics raises issues that go beyond the selection of a performant model. In official statistical production, AI systems must operate within an environment shaped by methodological standards, legal constraints, and institutional responsibilities. For this reason, their evaluation cannot be limited to generic machine learning metrics alone. Robust validation procedures are required, ideally based on annotated data and comparison with existing baselines, in order to assess not only predictive performance but also consistency, stability over time, and suitability for operational use. From the perspective of official statistics, statistical quality remains the key reference point: AI-based outputs must be sufficiently accurate, coherent, and controllable to support coding and classification activities without undermining the reliability of the final statistical product.

Additional constraints concern transparency, explainability, reproducibility, and auditability. Complex models based on embeddings or large language models may offer important gains in flexibility, but they can also make it more difficult to reconstruct how a classification output has been produced, especially when the workflow depends on external services, multiple processing stages, or dynamic model behavior. These concerns are particularly relevant in public-sector statistical production, where decisions must remain documentable and open to review. Moreover, the use of AI-based systems must be compatible with data protection and confidentiality requirements, and their integration into production must take into account existing statistical workflows, IT infrastructures, and organizational processes. Taken together, these issues show that AI for text classification cannot be governed only at the level of experimentation or model development. Its adoption requires a structured framework of rules, procedures, responsibilities, and safeguards capable of ensuring that innovation remains aligned with the quality principles and institutional obligations of official statistics. For this reason, the adoption of AI-based text classification at Istat must be supported by a governance framework capable of defining priorities, responsibilities, validation procedures, and operational safeguards for the design and deployment of AI applications.
