# Introduction

In Official Statistics, occupation classification provides the standardized framework needed to produce comparable and harmonized statistics on occupations. In Italy, this function is performed by CP2021, the national classification of professions, which represents the labour market through a hierarchical organization of occupational categories with increasing levels of specificity [CP2021] [CP2021Focus]. As the Italian version of ISCO, CP2021 also ensures coherence with the broader international framework for occupation statistics. At the same time, translating real-world descriptions of work activities into this formal classification remains a challenging task, since such descriptions are often heterogeneous, ambiguous, and expressed in natural language rather than in the institutional terminology of the taxonomy [AIgov2026] [TrustworthyAI2026].

This issue is particularly relevant in survey questionnaires that include open-ended questions whose responses must be coded into complex statistical classifications, such as those used for occupations or economic activities. The Labour Force Survey provides a clear example: respondents describe their main work activity in natural language, and these descriptions must then be translated into the official profession classification [FOL2026]. Such descriptions are often short, uneven in specificity, and expressed in everyday language rather than in the formal wording of CP2021. The central bottleneck is therefore semantic rather than merely lexical: the system must recover the intended occupational meaning even when overlap with the official class labels is weak or absent.

Istat already supports this task through the *Navigatore delle professioni*, a tool that combines hierarchical exploration of the classification with textual search over occupational entries [Navigatore2021]. Its methodology is essentially lexical and rule-based, since retrieval depends on matching normalized user queries against a controlled vocabulary built from CP2021 labels and the associated *voci professionali*, rather than on capturing semantic similarity between queries and occupational categories. These entries help bridge the gap between the formal taxonomy and the language commonly used by respondents, but they also create a significant maintenance burden: occupational vocabulary evolves over time, new professions emerge, and the supporting lexical resources must be continuously revised and expanded. The *Navigatore* is therefore an important institutional baseline, but it also highlights a structural limitation of lexical matching approaches: user queries and classification language do not always coincide.

More generally, the description of an individual's work may refer to several closely related dimensions, such as employment status, position in the profession, profession, and economic activity [CodificaNote]. In real responses, these dimensions are often intertwined. For this reason, they must be considered explicitly when designing a coding methodology. Coding should therefore be anchored to the activities actually performed rather than to the declared professional title alone, which may be generic, ambiguous, or misleading. In this context, the present paper formulates occupation coding as a semantic retrieval problem over a structured label space. More specifically, it evaluates whether the alignment between natural-language occupation descriptions and CP2021 classes can be improved by comparing different textual representations of 5-digit occupational codes. The contribution of the paper is therefore both methodological and practical: it benchmarks multilingual embedding models for retrieval-based occupation coding and shows that descriptor design is a major driver of performance improvements in this task [SemanticATECO2026] [LLMCoding2025].
