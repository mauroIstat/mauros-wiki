# Draft Introduction

Over the past decade, the environment in which national statistical institutes operate has changed profoundly, as the digitalization of society, the growing complexity of data ecosystems, and the rapid progress of Artificial Intelligence (AI) have reshaped how data is produced, accessed, and used. For national statistical institutes, the adoption of AI is not merely a technological choice, but also a governance issue that directly affects the integrity, reliability, and legitimacy of official statistical systems.

National statistical institutes operate in a context marked by rising expectations for timeliness and granularity, together with increasing pressure to make effective use of data already available within administrative and digital ecosystems. At the same time, the use of these data raises important questions related to methodological control, data governance, and legal compliance. In line with the risk-based approach promoted by the European AI Act, public authorities must ensure that the integration of such data into statistical production remains proportionate, purpose-limited, compatible with existing mandates, and compliant with established legal and institutional frameworks.

Structured classifications have always been central to official statistics, because they make it possible to transform heterogeneous phenomena into consistent and comparable statistical aggregates. The challenge arises when national statistical institutes need to assign precise classification codes to inputs that are not statistical by nature, such as user queries, enterprise descriptions, or textual content extracted from company websites. Automatic coding then becomes a key operational task linking unstructured textual evidence to formal statistical categories. However, the increasing volume, diversity, and dynamism of textual data challenge traditional rule-based systems, which often require extensive and costly maintenance and struggle to adapt to evolving terminology and new data sources. AI-based and machine learning approaches offer promising solutions to these limitations, enabling more scalable, adaptive, and efficient coding processes.

However, the adoption of AI within official statistics cannot be driven by technological potential alone. Statistical authorities operate under strict mandates: according to public-sector governance principles and emerging AI regulation, automated systems used in core statistical processes must ensure transparency, human oversight, traceability, and robustness. The use of AI in text-based coding tasks therefore requires careful governance to ensure that automated processes remain explainable, controllable, and aligned with statistical standards.

Innovation must remain consistent with the fundamental principles of official statistics. Quality, methodological soundness, confidentiality, and accountability remain essential requirements, grounded both in statistical law and in broader regulatory frameworks such as the AI Act. For this reason, AI-driven coding processes based on textual inputs must be developed within well-defined governance structures, supported by validation procedures, documentation, and risk mitigation measures. In this sense, automatic coding acts as a concrete use case through which national statistical institutes, including Istat, can test and operationalize trustworthy AI, ensuring that technological advancement reinforces, rather than undermines, the public value and credibility of official statistics.

Against this background, the aim of the paper is to present Istat's approach to AI-based text classification and automatic coding, combining methodological innovation with an emerging governance framework. Section 2 discusses AI and text classification in official statistics through two current use cases and highlights the methodological requirements associated with their adoption. Section 3 illustrates the regulatory and institutional bases, together with the main organizational and strategic elements, of Istat's AI governance framework. Concluding remarks and perspectives are presented in Section 4.
