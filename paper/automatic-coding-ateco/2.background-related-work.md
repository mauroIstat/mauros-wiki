# Background and Related Work

## 2.1 Automatic coding in Official Statistics

Automatic coding has long been a recurring problem in Official Statistics. National Statistical Institutes have repeatedly developed methods to support the assignment of official codes from textual descriptions in domains such as economic activities, occupations, and product classifications. This reflects a persistent operational need: reducing the burden of manual coding while preserving consistency, traceability, and comparability in the use of statistical classifications.

Historically, many of these systems have been based on deterministic and lexical matching strategies. Early work such as ACTR provided an important reference for automated coding in statistical production [ACTR; Appel and Hellerman]. In the Italian context, this line of development led to CIRCE, a rule-based system designed to assign classification codes from textual descriptions and used in practical coding workflows [CIRCE]. Systems of this kind are important not only because they automate part of the coding task, but also because they reflect methodological priorities that are central to OS, including transparency of the coding logic, expert control over the decision process, and operational stability over time.

At the same time, the strengths of rule-based systems are closely tied to their main limitation. Their performance depends on dictionaries, normalization procedures, synonym lists, and matching rules that must be explicitly designed and continuously maintained [CIRCE]. This creates a structural burden whenever classifications are revised, new forms of economic activity emerge, or users adopt wording that departs from the institutional terminology of the taxonomy. The issue is therefore not only predictive performance, but also the maintainability of the coding infrastructure itself.

This tension is particularly relevant for economic-activity classifications such as ATECO. Coding requires the interpretation of short and heterogeneous descriptions against a structured taxonomy whose distinctions are often subtle. In this context, rule-based approaches remain an important benchmark, but they also reveal the limits of purely lexical matching in the presence of paraphrases, ambiguous formulations, and evolving language.

## 2.2 Embedding-based semantic retrieval

Recent embedding models provide a different methodological perspective on coding support. Instead of relying mainly on lexical overlap, these models represent texts in a semantic vector space in which similarity is driven more by meaning than by exact wording [SBERT; BGE; mGTE]. This is particularly relevant when user queries and official class descriptors refer to the same activity through different expressions.

For the present problem, however, the key distinction is not only between rule-based and neural methods. It is also the distinction between classification and retrieval. In a standard classification setting, a model is trained to assign one label directly to an input text. In a retrieval setting, by contrast, the system compares the query with textual representations associated with the target categories and returns the most compatible candidates. This second formulation is especially natural when the operational objective is not a single fully automatic assignment, but a shortlist of plausible categories that can support coding decisions [SemanticATECO2026; LLMCoding2025].

This distinction matters because economic-activity coding does not depend only on how queries are represented, but also on how the target classification is expressed in semantic terms. Official classifications are not flat inventories of labels. They include titles, hierarchical relations, inclusion and exclusion notes, and other textual elements that help define the meaning of each category. As a result, representing the categories of the classification is itself part of the methodological problem rather than a secondary implementation detail. Different strategies are possible: a category may be represented through a single textual formulation, through several textual components that capture different aspects of its definition, or through a synthetic representation constructed from multiple pieces of text. These alternatives reflect different assumptions about how the semantic content of a statistical category should be modeled.

For this reason, embedding-based coding should not be seen simply as the replacement of one prediction engine with another. Its scientific interest lies in the possibility of treating the representation of the classification itself as an explicit object of methodological design.

## 2.3 Challenges in real-world coding

Real-world coding tasks introduce constraints that are only partially captured by standard text classification settings. Queries are often short, underspecified, and expressed in everyday language rather than in the controlled wording of the official taxonomy. In addition, coding difficulty is not uniform across the classification, since some divisions or categories are inherently more ambiguous or harder to distinguish than others. These features make economic-activity coding a problem in which shortlist quality, robustness across subgroups, and operational usability matter alongside exact prediction.
