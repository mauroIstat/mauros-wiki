# Introduction

Over the past decade, the environment in which National Statistical Institutes (NSIs) operate has changed significantly. Data ecosystems have become more complex, and expectations for Official Statistics (OS) have grown in terms of timeliness, accessibility, and analytical detail. At the same time, NSIs operate within methodological and institutional constraints that are essential to preserve the public value of official data, including reliability, transparency, and comparability over time and across domains. In this context, the modernization of statistical production requires not only methodological innovation, but also careful evaluation of how new methods fit within the principles and quality requirements of OS.

In OS, statistical classifications play a central role. Classifications such as NACE, ISCO, and PRODCOM, together with their national implementations, provide the structured vocabulary through which economic and social phenomena are observed, recorded, and integrated into statistical systems. In Italy, the reference classification for economic activities is ATECO, which organizes economic activities into a hierarchical taxonomy with progressively finer levels of detail. This structure is essential because it provides a common framework for describing economic activities.

This role becomes particularly visible in survey questionnaires that include open-ended questions, in which respondents are asked to describe their economic activity in natural language and the resulting responses must then be translated into the official ATECO classification. Such descriptions are often brief, uneven in specificity, and expressed in everyday language rather than in the controlled wording of the classification. Moreover, ATECO does not consist only of code titles. It also includes hierarchical relations, explanatory notes, inclusions, and exclusions that are crucial for distinguishing among nearby categories. The coding problem is therefore semantic rather than merely lexical: the task is not simply to detect a matching keyword, but to recover the intended economic activity even when the wording of the query only partially overlaps with the institutional language of the taxonomy.

To support this task, NSIs have historically relied on automated coding tools based on deterministic rules and lexical matching. In the Italian case, the main institutional reference is CIRCE, a rule-based system designed to assign classification codes from textual descriptions. Systems of this kind have important strengths. They are transparent, operationally familiar, and effective when the vocabulary is sufficiently covered by curated dictionaries, normalization rules, and synonym resources. They also provide a stable methodological baseline for coding activities. However, these systems exhibit a structural limitation: their performance depends heavily on the continued maintenance of dictionaries and matching rules. When classifications are revised, new forms of economic activity emerge, or users adopt different wording, substantial manual updates are required. The transition from ATECO 2022 to ATECO 2025 provides a concrete example of this burden.

These limitations motivate the exploration of more flexible approaches to coding support. Recent advances in language modelling, and especially in sentence-level embedding models, make it possible to represent texts in a semantic vector space where similarity is driven less by exact lexical overlap and more by meaning. In this setting, the descriptions associated with classification codes and the free-text queries produced by users can be embedded in a common representation space, allowing candidate codes to be retrieved according to semantic proximity. For official statistics, the interest of this approach is not only technical. Semantic retrieval has the potential to reduce dependence on manually curated lexical rules, improve robustness to paraphrases and wording variation, and support the modernization of coding systems under realistic operational constraints.

At the same time, automatic coding in official statistics should not be framed as a pure top-1 classification problem. In many practical settings, coding systems may support the extraction of statistical information from non-traditional textual sources, expert review, processes in which users are guided toward the most appropriate code for their enterprise. For this reason, the present paper formulates automatic coding of economic activities as a ranking problem over the ATECO label space. More specifically, it studies whether embedding-based semantic retrieval can provide operationally useful candidate sets for 5-digit ATECO categories, while remaining compatible with the practical requirements of NSIs, including operational feasibility, maintainability, and interpretability of the coding workflow.

The contribution of the paper is both methodological and applied. It evaluates whether embedding-based retrieval can provide operationally useful support for coding in OS, with particular attention to practical deployment, representation choices, model size, and the constraints of official statistical production.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the background on automatic coding in official statistics, semantic retrieval, and the practical challenges posed by real-world coding tasks. Section 3 formulates the problem and makes explicit its ranking-based interpretation. Section 4 presents the methodological framework, including the alternative representation strategies and evaluation criteria. Section 5 describes the data sources used in the study, while Section 6 details the experimental design. Section 7 reports the empirical results, with particular attention to overall retrieval quality, robustness across divisions, and the role of query length and similarity thresholds. Section 8 discusses the implications of the findings for official statistics and outlines the main limitations of the current setting. Section 9 concludes.
