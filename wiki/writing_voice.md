# Writing Voice

## General profile

The scientific voice in the corpus is compact, institutional, and methodologically cautious. It is generally formal without being ornamental. The prose is explanatory and applied, with repeated attention to why a method matters for Official Statistics rather than only how it works technically.

## Typical sentence-level tendencies

- Open with structural context: changing data ecosystems, rising expectations, new data sources, or AI advances.
- Use balanced pairs such as innovation and trust, opportunity and challenge, flexibility and rigor, efficiency and quality.
- Prefer verbs like support, enable, improve, preserve, align, integrate, and examine over stronger promotional verbs.
- Reuse institutional vocabulary: methodological soundness, quality frameworks, transparency, confidentiality, validation, public trust, responsible adoption.
- Introduce technical components in plain academic language before adding formulas or implementation detail.

## What the voice avoids

- Overclaiming novelty.
- Startup-like language about disruption.
- Purely technical framing detached from institutional consequences.
- Generic AI enthusiasm that is not tied to validation, quality, or official use.

## Distinction between scientific and teaching voice

The teaching material is more direct, intuitive, and audience-facing. It uses rhetorical questions, everyday examples, and simple contrasts such as "the real world is messy" or "what if machines could learn the rules?" The scientific material is more restrained and cumulative: it explains concepts clearly, but does so through methodological motivation and institutional framing.
