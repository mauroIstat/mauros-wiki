# Automatically Coding Economic Activities via Semantic Search

## Abstract

Official economic activity classifications provide the reference framework for producing coherent and comparable statistics, yet their complexity makes accurate self-classification challenging. National Statistical Institutes (NSIs) have traditionally relied on rule-based systems like CIRCE, which require extensive manual maintenance and struggle with synonyms or paraphrases. We present an alternative approach based on semantic search with sentence-transformer embeddings, comparing naïve, disentangled, and centroid-based knowledge bases. Using more than 33,000 CIRCE-labelled queries and 12,400 synthetic queries, we evaluate various embedding models across different levels of the ATECO hierarchy. Results indicate that semantic search methods achieve over 80% top-5 matching at the 5-digit level, making them suitable for interactive coding tools. We further demonstrate that medium-sized models provide strong performance while remaining suitable for offline deployment in survey environments. These findings support transitioning from ATECO 2022 to ATECO 2025 and highlight the potential of embedding-based techniques to modernize automated coding in official statistics.

## Keywords

Automated coding, Semantic search, Embedding models, Statistical classifications

## Introduction

Statistical classifications such as NACE, ISCO, PRODCOM, and their national variations, like ATECO in Italy, are essential tools for Official Statistics (OS). They provide a common language for describing economic and social phenomena, ensuring coherence and comparability across countries, different data sources, and statistical domains. With standardised categories, classifications enable the integration of survey data, administrative records, and business registers into consistent statistical frameworks, thereby supporting both national and international policymaking. Accurate classification is essential as misreporting can lead to biased estimates, reduced comparability, and inconsistencies in national accounts and business statistics.

However, navigating these taxonomies can be challenging. Classifications are hierarchical and include many explanatory notes, which, while useful for domain experts, may confuse users without specialised knowledge. For enterprises and respondents filling out survey questionnaires or administrative forms, finding the correct code often requires substantial effort. This burden can lead to incorrect self-reporting and higher costs for both respondents and statistical offices.

To address these challenges, National Statistical Institutes have developed auxiliary systems to support classification tasks. A key example is the Italian Statistical Institute's Comprehensive ISTAT R Coding Environment (CIRCE), a rule-based search engine designed to help retrieve economic activity codes from natural-language queries. These systems have enhanced the quality of coding tasks and reduced respondent burden. Nevertheless, their deterministic design has significant limitations: rule sets need continuous manual updates whenever the classification is revised, and the lack of contextual understanding limits their ability to generalize, especially with synonyms, complex phrasing, or typographical errors.

The rapid progress in Artificial Intelligence, especially with Transformer-based language models, creates new opportunities to modernize coding processes. Unlike traditional rule-based systems, which rely on explicit keyword matching and manual rules, these models process and represent semantics. This allows them to interpret meaning beyond lexical matches and enables the development of search engines that can identify when different sentences convey similar meaning, handle synonyms and paraphrases, and understand queries even if they contain typos or unusual phrasing. As a result, Transformer-based methods significantly increase generalization capabilities and help systems manage the variability and ambiguity of natural language while considerably reducing the need for manual rule updates.

The paper explores the use of semantic search techniques for coding economic activities. Building on Istat's experience with CIRCE, it proposes an approach that uses Sentence Transformers to generate text embeddings of classification descriptions and user queries, enabling similarity-based retrieval of relevant codes. The method is compared with the CIRCE baseline using tens of thousands of real-world queries, while synthetic queries generated by a large language model are used to enrich the knowledge base.

The contribution is presented as threefold:

1. Design and evaluation of three alternative embedding-based knowledge bases for semantic search, ranging from naïve to centroid-based approaches.
2. Benchmarking against CIRCE using more than 33,000 real-world labelled queries, complemented with synthetic data generated by LLMs.
3. Demonstration that semantic search methods can reduce manual maintenance costs and increase robustness to synonyms, paraphrases, and typos, supporting the transition to the next ATECO revision.

The submitted manuscript is centered on ATECO 2007, revision 2022, because the current CIRCE system and the associated labelled query dataset are based on that version. The text explicitly notes that the approach can be extended to ATECO 2025.

## CIRCE

NSIs have developed automated coding systems for decades. Automatic Coding by Text Recognition (ACTR) was employed in official statistics applications, including national population censuses. CIRCE was developed at Istat as a generalised software package designed to assign classification codes based on textual descriptions. Introduced as a successor to ACTR v3, CIRCE was implemented in R to ensure portability across platforms and long-term sustainability. The system is classification- and language-independent, allowing application across several coding contexts.

In practice, CIRCE has been used especially for ATECO, supporting both businesses and internal statistical activities. CIRCE combines parsing routines with rule-based weighting algorithms to offer a reliable coding method, reducing manual work and ensuring greater consistency. However, each revision of the classification requires significant human effort to update rule sets, synonyms, and dictionaries. The release of ATECO 2025 is presented as a concrete example of this maintenance burden.

The paper uses these limitations as the main rationale for experimenting with semantic search methods based on embedding models and large language models.

## Data

The study relies on three data components:

1. The ATECO classification.
2. CIRCE-labelled real-world queries.
3. Synthetic queries generated with GPT-5-mini.

Together, these sources are used to evaluate both empirical alignment with observed user behaviour and broader lexical coverage in controlled settings.

### ATECO Classification

The ATECO classification groups together economic activities that are assumed to be similar at various levels, respecting the principles of completeness and exhaustiveness. Categories are mutually exclusive, and the hierarchy is made of six levels:

- Sections: single-letter codes from A to U
- Divisions: 2-digit numeric codes from 01 to 99
- Groups: 3-digit numeric codes
- Classes: 4-digit numeric codes
- Categories: 5-digit numeric codes
- Sub-categories: 6-digit numeric codes

The paper reports the following hierarchy sizes:

| Level | Digits | Size | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Section | 1 | 21 | A, U |
| Division | 2 | 87 | 05, 73 |
| Group | 3 | 270 | 46.7, 74.2 |
| Class | 4 | 615 | 46.73 |
| Category | 5 | 919 | 46.73.4 |
| Sub-category | 6 | 1240 | 46.73.40 |

Beyond titles and numeric codes, ATECO contains central notes, inclusion notes, exclusion notes, and operational notes. The paper argues that this richness helps human coding but also makes automation difficult, because the correct assignment often depends on subtle distinctions expressed in natural language.

The manuscript also reports a strongly unbalanced distribution of active businesses across ATECO divisions. Division 47 (retail trade) is the largest, while Division 99 is almost empty. This imbalance is treated as relevant context for model design and evaluation.

### CIRCE-Labelled Real-World Queries

The real-world dataset consists of 33,544 user queries collected through the CIRCE-based platform used by businesses and professionals to identify ATECO categories from natural-language descriptions. Each query is passed through CIRCE to obtain an ATECO category at the 5-digit level, producing a labelled dataset used as the reference benchmark.

The paper uses this dataset to measure alignment between semantic methods and the traditional rule-based baseline. It also notes that the distribution of CIRCE-assigned labels across divisions loosely resembles the broader distribution of enterprises across the taxonomy.

### Synthetic Queries

In addition to real-world queries, the paper generates a synthetic dataset using GPT-5-mini. The model is instructed to create ten short queries for each ATECO sub-category, producing 12,400 synthetic queries in total.

For each 6-digit sub-category, the prompt includes a descriptor that combines:

- title
- description
- inclusion notes
- exclusion notes
- hierarchy information

The paper explains that these synthetic queries are meant to support broader lexical coverage and to help build centroid-based knowledge bases. Because CIRCE labels exist at the 5-digit category level while the synthetic queries are generated at the 6-digit level, the synthetic data are aggregated to the 5-digit parent category for evaluation. Specifically, all synthetic queries from the child sub-categories are combined and their embeddings are used to compute a single 5-digit centroid.

## Methodology

The methodological section is organized around three components:

1. traditional rule-based methods such as CIRCE
2. sentence transformers as the embedding backbone
3. alternative knowledge-base construction strategies

### Traditional Rule-Based Methods

Traditional approaches to automatic coding in official statistics are described as deterministic systems based on rule sets and weighted keyword matching. Text is parsed through tokenisation, stop-word removal, synonym replacement, and other normalization strategies. Dictionary entries derived from the classification are represented as weighted token sets. User queries undergo the same parsing procedure and are then matched against the dictionary.

CIRCE produces four outcomes depending on the best score, the second-best score, and fixed thresholds:

1. `Unique`
2. `Multiple`
3. `Possible`
4. `Failed`

The paper highlights the strengths of this architecture, especially transparency and expert control, but stresses four limitations:

- costly maintenance of parsing strategies and synonym lists
- no semantic understanding beyond token overlap
- rigid matching logic
- poor scalability across new classifications or domains

### Sentence Transformers

The semantic approach relies on sentence transformers to encode texts into dense sentence-level embeddings. The paper frames the target representation as a `knowledge base`, where each ATECO code is represented as one vector or a set of vectors.

The embedding models tested are:

| Model | Parameters | Vector size |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Qwen-Embedding-8B | 8,000 million | 4,096 |
| Qwen-Embedding-4B | 4,000 million | 2,560 |
| Qwen-Embedding-0.6B | 596 million | 1,024 |
| BGE-M3 | 568 million | 1,024 |
| gte-multilingual-base | 305 million | 768 |
| EmbeddingGemma | 303 million | 768 |
| paraphrase-multilingual | 118 million | 384 |

### Knowledge-Base Design Strategies

The paper compares three main strategies:

#### Naïve approach

Each code is represented by a single consolidated descriptor. The query embedding is compared with one embedding per code through cosine similarity, and top-k codes are retrieved.

#### Disentangled approach

Each code is represented by multiple sub-texts. These can be extracted from the official classification or from synthetic queries. Each sub-text is embedded separately, and the final score for a code is obtained with max pooling over the code-specific similarity scores.

#### Centroid-based approach

Each code is represented by the average embedding of its sub-texts. In practice, the code embedding is the centroid of the official sub-texts or synthetic queries associated with that code. Retrieval is then performed as in the naïve approach, using cosine similarity against one centroid per code.

### Evaluation Setup

The paper evaluates alignment between semantic retrieval and CIRCE labels at two hierarchy levels:

- ATECO divisions (2-digit)
- ATECO categories (5-digit)

Three ranking metrics are reported:

- `match@1`
- `match@3`
- `match@5`

The 2-digit metrics are obtained by truncating the retrieved 5-digit categories to their first two digits.

## Results

### Overall Comparison

The central quantitative result is reported through a large comparison table across:

- model
- knowledge-base strategy
- source of sub-texts
- ranking level

The paper reports the best category-level top-5 performance for the largest model in the centroid-synthetic setting and identifies medium-sized models as the most realistic trade-off for operational use.

Selected values from the table are:

| Approach | Model | Division Match@5 | Category Match@5 |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: |
| Naïve | Qwen-Embedding-8B | 0.8657 | 0.7184 |
| Disentangled (official) | gte-multilingual-base | 0.8957 | 0.7671 |
| Centroid (official) | gte-multilingual-base | 0.8881 | 0.7684 |
| Centroid (synthetic) | Qwen-Embedding-8B | 0.9139 | 0.8215 |
| Centroid (synthetic) | Qwen-Embedding-4B | 0.9138 | 0.8207 |
| Centroid (synthetic) | BGE-M3 | 0.8831 | 0.7689 |

The text draws four main conclusions from the table:

1. The naïve approach is generally sub-optimal, especially for smaller models.
2. Disentangled and centroid-based strategies are more promising than a single-descriptor representation.
3. Synthetic queries help in some settings but not uniformly across all models.
4. Medium-sized models such as `gte-multilingual-base` and `BGE-M3` provide the best trade-off between performance and feasibility for production environments.

### Matching by Division

The paper then examines average `match@5` by 2-digit division, using `gte-multilingual-base` with the centroid-based approach over official sub-texts. No division falls below 50%. The weakest divisions are reported as:

- 13: textile industries
- 25: manufacture of metal products, excluding machinery and equipment

The text notes that underperformance is not stable across models. For example, `BGE-M3` underperforms substantially on division 43, whereas `gte-multilingual-base` performs much better there. This variability is interpreted as evidence that model performance depends on training data and task fit, not only on model size.

### Similarity Thresholds

The paper studies the effect of imposing a cosine-similarity threshold before assigning a code. Raising the threshold increases top-1 matching among the classified cases but reduces the proportion of queries that receive a label. The manuscript notes that different models exhibit different trade-offs, and that raw cosine scores are not directly comparable across models.

### Semantic Richness and Performance

One of the most practically important findings concerns query length. The paper filters the CIRCE dataset to the subset of queries with at least five words, leaving 12,518 queries out of the original 33,544. On this semantically richer subset, performance improves consistently.

For the centroid-official setting:

| Model | Query filter | Division Match@5 | Category Match@5 |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: |
| BGE-M3 | Standard | 0.8691 | 0.7350 |
| BGE-M3 | `> 4` words | 0.9264 | 0.8070 |
| gte-multilingual-base | Standard | 0.8881 | 0.7684 |
| gte-multilingual-base | `> 4` words | 0.9394 | 0.8185 |

The interpretation is explicit: semantic methods benefit from richer descriptions more than rule-based systems do.

### A Note on Mismatches

The manuscript manually inspects a sample of strong mismatches, defined as cases where CIRCE and semantic retrieval disagree already at the 2-digit division level. The paper reports that many such cases are ambiguous rather than clear errors. Some examples suggest that the semantic approach occasionally retrieves a more plausible label than CIRCE, while in other cases CIRCE remains more convincing. This is used to argue that CIRCE labels are a useful benchmark but not a perfect gold standard.

## Conclusion

The conclusion presents semantic search as a viable path for automated coding of economic activities in official statistics. The paper argues that:

- modern embedding-based approaches can achieve high shortlist quality
- medium-sized models are operationally attractive because they can support offline or light-infrastructure deployment
- semantic methods sharply reduce the need for manually maintained rules

The practical scenario emphasized most strongly is interactive coding support, where users select from a shortlist rather than rely on a single automatic label. The paper also discusses future work on similarity thresholds, alternative classification strategies, conformal prediction, expert validation, fine-tuning, and the transition to ATECO 2025. It closes by noting that code and public reproducibility materials are available, while the CIRCE queries themselves cannot be released because they may contain sensitive information.
